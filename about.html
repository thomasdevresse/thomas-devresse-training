Why I coach this way

I started training because I lacked confidence.

What kept me in the gym was not appearance. It was the clarity of measurable progress.

I was very thin and self-conscious about my physique when I first stepped into a gym in 2015. Building confidence got me through the door; performance gave me a reason to stay. For much of that time, I trained primarily for strength.

Progress was not linear. Private setbacks, the COVID pause and a persistent shoulder issue repeatedly interrupted it. That experience made me more deliberate about movement, load management and exercise selection. It also reinforced an important boundary: coaching can adapt training and build capacity, while diagnosis and treatment belong to qualified healthcare professionals.

Rehab-U and my later education in assessment and program design changed the questions I ask as a coach: which movements are available today, where does control break down, and how should training progress? That search became one of the foundations of my approach.

From practice to profession

From personal practice to full-time coaching.

Training taught me discipline and experimentation. Management taught me to listen, lead and teach. Independent coaching brought those lessons together.

  1. Jan 2019

    Fitness Coach at Jims

    I joined part-time at reception and on the gym floor, intending to develop personal training alongside the role.

  2. Sep 2019

    Promoted to Club Manager

    The opportunity delayed my PT plan, but accelerated my understanding of people. Daily contact with members, trainers and group instructors taught me how different needs, fears and motivations shape a fitness journey. Training the team also strengthened my interest in teaching.

  3. Apr 2022

    Independent coach, part-time

    I began personal training independently while continuing to manage the club full-time.

  4. Jul 2023

    Full-time independent coach

    I left management to focus entirely on independent personal coaching. Continued education and field experience have since made assessment increasingly central to my approach.

The Kaizen method

Small steps. Strong foundations. Progress that holds

Kaizen gave a name to what experience had already taught me: well-chosen steps compound more reliably than oversized leaps followed by repeated setbacks.

I learned that by trying to move too fast myself. Today, the goal is not caution for its own sake. It is to make each demanding step productive—and to build enough understanding for clients to keep making better decisions with less supervision.
